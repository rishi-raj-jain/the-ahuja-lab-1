Dr. Gaurav Ahuja

Assistant Professor (Computational Biology)
PhD (2015), University of Cologne, Germany

Gaurav Ahuja graduated summa cum laude from the University of Cologne, Germany with PhD in Natural Sciences. He is a molecular biologist by training and possesses a vast experience in functional genomics, especially in dissecting complex biological mechanisms using genome-editing and next generation sequencing-based techniques. After the completion of his PhD degree, he worked as a Post Doctoral Fellow at several renowned institutes, including Center for Molecular Medicine Cologne, Max Planck Institute for Ageing and Lee Kong Chian School of Medicine, Singapore. During this period, he worked on multiple model systems, including human pluripotent stem cells, zebrafish, mice and killifish.

Our Interest

Essential cellular behaviors, including those that ensure its survival or homeostasis, are modulated by many intrinsic or extrinsic factors. For the last few decades, efforts have been ongoing to identify these factors, followed by their functional characterization, ultimately leading to decoding these complex response mechanisms. Despite years of experimentation involving heating, boiling, freezing, and electroporation of cells, our understanding of how these chemical moieties influence vital fundamental cellular processes is far from complete.

The Ahuja Lab at IIIT-Delhi uses a hybrid approach involving Artificial Intelligence and Molecular Biology. Some of the long-term projects of the lab include:

    Project 1: Dissecting the roles of intrinsic metabolites using Artificial Intelligence and yeast-based high-throughput bioassays

    Cellular functionalities and their underlying complexities are historically investigated around the central dogma of molecular biology. Recent advancements in high throughput metabolomics and their functional validations uncovered the hidden potential of metabolites, a class of chemical compounds that act as a substrate or product of metabolism and drive essential cellular functions. The metabolic diversity of any organism is highly complex because of parallel metabolite contributions from exogenous sources such as microorganisms, xenobiotics, dietary, etc. Our laboratory aims to dissect some of the fundamental aspects of metabolites' functional contributions in driving essential cellular processes or behaviors. We achieve this by using state-of-the-art machine learning algorithms with explainability modules coupled with high-throughput bioassays to cross-validate AI predictions. Furthermore, we also utilize RNA sequencing or proteomics techniques to gain deeper mechanistic insights. Notably, to ensure the relevance of the identified mechanisms in evolutionarily advanced species, we also test our key findings using primary or immortalized human cell lines.


    Project 2: Artificial Intelligence for Anti-Aging drug discovery

    Aging involves a temporal decline in cellular functionality. Over the last few decades, almost all areas of omics technologies such as genomics, transcriptomics, epigenomics, metabolomics, proteomics, pharmacogenomics, and metagenomics have been tested in the hunt for the identification of master regulators of aging. The Ahuja Lab at IIIT-Delhi leverages Artificial Intelligence (AI) to discover aging biomarkers, critical molecular targets, and anti-aging drugs. We also devised an array of novel, yeast-based high-throughput assays targeting "Hallmarks of Aging."


    Project 3: Decoding Olfaction (sense of smell) within and outside Nose

    Biological systems harbor the ability to selectively and precisely receive inputs from various intrinsic or extrinsic clues. These stimuli trigger a transient modulation of distinct gene regulatory networks by the combinatorial effect of genetic and epigenetic signatures, ultimately leading to complex physiological or behavioral outputs. The underlying diversity and complexity of the processes above highlight the core research interest of our laboratory.


Some relevant questions that trigger our laboratory interest include:

  • What genetic or epigenetic factors govern selective OR expression in the malignant cells?
  • What are their functional contributions?
  • If they impart chemosensory function, what is the triggering stimulus for these ORs within the tumor microenvironment?
  • Since multiple ORs are co-expressed within a single malignant cell, then how do the malignant cells manage the highly convoluted downstream pathways and their potential cross-talks?
  • If ORs are indeed multifunctional genes in the human genome, then why are a significant proportion of them pseudogenized or under negative selection pressure?
  • Which molecular features define the tuning ability (narrowly or broadly tuned) or ligand specificity of the OR?


To achieve this, The Ahuja Lab effectively takes advantage of effectively takes advantage of both chemoinformatics, molecular biology, and genomics techniques.
